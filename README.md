Overview
This model analyzes the $7.1 billion take-private leveraged buyout of BMC Software, Inc. by a sponsor consortium consisting of Bain Capital, Golden Gate Capital, Insight Venture Partners, and GIC. The transaction was one of the largest technology buyouts of 2013.
The model is built from scratch and covers the full LBO framework, from transaction assumptions and capital structure through to a five-year operating projection, debt paydown schedule, and sponsor return analysis across three scenarios.

Company Background
BMC Software was a leading provider of enterprise IT management software serving large multinational corporations across infrastructure, cloud, and service management. At the time of the transaction, the company generated approximately $2.2 billion in annual revenue, with a high proportion of recurring maintenance and license fees underpinning a predictable and defensible revenue base.
Key characteristics that made BMC an attractive LBO candidate:

High switching costs and deeply embedded customer relationships
Consistent EBITDA margins of approximately 31.5%
Minimal capital intensity (CapEx at 1.1% of revenue)
Strong free cash flow conversion supporting aggressive debt service

Key Risks
Multiple compression — The return profile is highly sensitive to exit multiple assumptions. A two-turn compression from 10.3x to 8.0x at exit reduces IRR to 0.2% and MOIC to barely above 1.0x, representing a near-total loss of sponsor return.
Cloud substitution risk — The 2013–2018 hold period coincides with accelerating enterprise cloud adoption. BMC's traditional on-premise IT management software faces structural headwinds as customers migrate workloads to AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud, potentially pressuring both revenue growth and renewal rates beyond the model's 4.0% base case assumption.
High leverage leaving limited downside protection — Entry leverage of 7.9x EBITDA with an additional second lien tranche at 10.0% leaves minimal margin for operational underperformance. A sustained revenue decline or margin compression would rapidly erode free cash flow and threaten debt service capacity, particularly in the early years of the hold period when the debt burden is heaviest.

Investment Thesis
The BMC transaction is a classic defensive LBO thesis built on three pillars:
1. Recurring revenue visibility — Multi-year maintenance contracts with large institutional customers create high revenue predictability throughout the hold period. High switching costs associated with deeply embedded IT management software create natural barriers to churn.
2. Strong free cash flow conversion — The combination of a 31.5% EBITDA margin and minimal capital intensity (1.1% CapEx/revenue) produces approximately $331M of free cash flow in year one, growing to over $473M by year five as operating leverage compounds.
3. Compelling deleveraging story — Entry leverage of 7.9x EBITDA declines meaningfully over the five-year hold period as excess cash flow is swept against the Term Loan B. Debt paydown alone contributes $2.5B of equity value creation in the base case.
